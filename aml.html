Anti-Money Laundering (AML)

AML program structure, typologies, transaction monitoring rules, and alert management.
Legal Basis
The AML program is required under the Bank Secrecy Act (31 USC 5318(h)). Willful failure to maintain an adequate AML program is a criminal offense under 31 USC 5322, punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment and penalties up to $250,000 per violation.

The Three Stages of Money Laundering

Understanding the laundering cycle is foundational to detection. All three stages must be covered by monitoring controls:

StageDescriptionCommon Indicators
Placement Introduction of illicit cash into the financial system Structured cash deposits, round-amount transactions, unusual cash intensity
Layering Concealing the trail through complex transactions Rapid fund transfers, shell company activity, wire layering, crypto conversion
Integration Reintroduction of laundered funds into the legitimate economy Real estate purchases, luxury asset acquisition, loan-back schemes

Transaction Monitoring System (TMS)

The organization's Transaction Monitoring System (TMS) applies rule-based and machine learning detection to identify potentially suspicious activity. Alerts are generated when transaction patterns breach defined thresholds or behavioral models flag anomalies.

Rule Tuning
All TMS rules must be reviewed semi-annually. Threshold changes require written justification, back-testing evidence, and sign-off by the BSA Officer. Rule suppressions exceeding 30 days require Compliance Committee approval.

Alert Disposition Workflow

All TMS alerts follow a standardized disposition process:

  1. Alert Generation — TMS creates an alert based on rule or model trigger.
  2. L1 Review (Analyst) — Analyst reviews within 5 business days. Dispositions: Close (no suspicious activity), Escalate, or Request Additional Information.
  3. L2 Review (Senior Analyst) — Escalated alerts reviewed within 5 business days. Dispositions: Close with documentation, Escalate to L3, or File SAR.
  4. L3 Review (Compliance Officer) — Final disposition for complex cases. SAR filing decision made here.
  5. SAR Filing — Filed within 30 calendar days of initial detection of suspicious activity. 90-day extension available with documentation.
Tipping Off Prohibition
Federal law (31 USC 5318(g)(2)) prohibits disclosing to any person involved in a SAR-reported transaction that a SAR has been filed or is being considered. Violation is a criminal offense.
